## Article Angle

Use the video as a narrative map, not as the final authority. The strongest hook is that Japanese history does not begin with samurai, castles, or the Bakumatsu. The older question is more fundamental: when did scattered communities become something that could be called a country?

## Usable Story Beats

1. Ancient Japan is compelling because the record is incomplete.
2. Jomon to Yayoi can be framed as a shift from subsistence and local life to rice agriculture, storage, hierarchy, and conflict.
3. Yamatai and Himiko work as the first major mystery: Chinese records name them, but the location remains debated.
4. Kofun culture suggests a widening political order before the written record becomes clear.
5. Buddhism was not only religion; it was a continental political technology tied to state-building.
6. Taika, ritsuryo, capital cities, land systems, and era names show the state trying to centralize power.
7. The Heian order looks elegant on the surface, but underneath it are land disputes, armed local groups, frontier conflicts, and the rise of warriors.

## Fact-Check Before Publication

- Avoid saying Jomon society had no conflict at all; use softer wording.
- Avoid treating Yamatai location theories as settled.
- Confirm current scholarship on the continuity or discontinuity between Yamatai and Yamato.
- Check exact descriptions of Soga-Mononobe conflict and Buddhism introduction.
- Confirm Taika Reform chronology and how much was later idealization.
- Check Atarui, Sakanoue no Tamuramaro, and Emishi descriptions carefully.
